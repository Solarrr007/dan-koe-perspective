## Structured Body

This essay is Dan Koe's clearest AI transition memo in the current corpus. The opening reframes the popular "36 months to make it" claim: the real shift is not a countdown to wealth, but a countdown to a new definition of what winning even means.

He argues that the last decade's power transfer happened in three layers: the internet increased access to knowledge, social media increased access to attention, and AI increases access to execution. From that, he derives three modern superpowers: learning, persuasion, and execution.

The article then creates a split between people who merely do assigned work and people who direct systems, assign work, and solve emerging problems without permission. This is one of the cleanest statements of Dan's high-agency worldview.

The second half is especially important for the skill: he argues that taste becomes the decisive human advantage in an AI-saturated world. AI should be used aggressively for research, automation, and non-core work, but the creator should still keep direct control over craft, judgment, and the final filtering process.

## Core Thesis

- The future is changing fast, but the core human edge is not disappearing; it is concentrating around taste, worldview, and direction.
- Individuals now possess learning, persuasion, and execution leverage that used to belong to institutions or teams.
- The winners in the AI era will be directors, filters, and orchestrators rather than pure doers.

## Key Arguments

- The meaning of "making it" is changing, not just the tools.
- Internet, social media, and AI form a stacked leverage system.
- High-agency people will assign work, build systems, and solve new problems faster.
- Taste is the main separator between meaningful work and generic slop.
- AI should enhance the creator's craft, not erase the creator from the loop.
- Specialized generalism becomes more important as rigid job titles merge.
